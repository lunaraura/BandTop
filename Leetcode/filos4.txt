Although it may be denied that  the encapsulating entirety as we know is false, it is not deniable that experience does not exist. In what necessitates the world as we know, there are actual truths compared to the standard statements that are bound to ontology. Within ontology are methodologies of inference towards things that, in the first order, are not verified or discovered through intuition and sense and cannot be done so. There are, in higher orders, things that simply cannot be understood without complex relations and abstract structures and systems and the knowledge of these (ontological) objects.
This is not to say that truth does not exist outside of the subject, yet, an ontological truth is different from the phenomenological truth. Through what is given, the information of the world is appearing to consciousness: this is a self-evident process. The fact of cognition, the unconscious, and the ego, id, and superego as an example are all products of discovery and concept. Ontology however is not yet a result of the exploration of experience. Ontology becomes a culmination of sciences between the correspondence of the represented object and how it will interact with other objects. While an ontology can be built by a lone subject studying and observing the world, there exists a fair amount of error. In what appears to correspond with the truth-claims and objects will have at least minimal error in perspective of the subject. To the perspective of some, in example, a person can know of an apple but not the nutritional contents of it and another can know of nutrition and the mechanics of digestion but not of an apple or orange. There are missing pieces of knowledge in each subject and some have more missing than others will. It may be posited that the ontological truth, one statement towards a property or object which will correspond as the statement provides without failure, could be an actual correspondence to metaphysics although this may be a stretch. Beyond physicality, natural science, and such, a statement has the possibility of no longer upholding an "objective" truth. To say that entropy will move towards a constant when the temperature approaches zero is different to the claim that someone's shirt is red or that lions are of the Felidae family. The former of the second group is of an observation of qualia and the second is of a statement of made-up classifications where both may be true in a sense but not some kind of "universal" fact. The substrate that has been conceptually created for ontology (physics, particles, etc) may be a result of transcending conditions from metaphysics itself.
Here in the phenomenal "domain" (and it is a stretch to say "domain" as ontology, metaphysics, and our minds aren't very reducible into sets, or at least I do not know of a way to reduce every idea and concept into a set of sets), there will be a "subjectness" and "experience" along with the potential for willpower. Most of what can be said about consciousness, cognition, and the mind right now is circulated in ontological concepts and ideas of the outside, so much so that it is nearly impossible to separate from what is fundamental to experience-itself and what seems to be ontologically fundamental for the mind, yet is deducted by experts such as Husserl, Heidegger, and such.

What I Need to See to Understand the Positions
The core tension across all three drafts is that you're operating with several key terms that shift meaning without announcement. Before anything else, I need to see you commit to stable definitions of:
Experience vs. consciousness vs. cognition. You use these sometimes interchangeably, sometimes as distinct orders. Are you treating consciousness as a product of cognition, or cognition as accessible only through consciousness? The answer changes everything downstream.
Transcendence vs. correspondence. You introduced "transcendental correspondence" as a careful alternative to correlation, but then use transcendence loosely throughout. What precisely is the relationship you're positing between metaphysical contingencies and phenomenological foundations — is it causal, structural, analogical?
The ideal domain. This phrase carries enormous weight across the drafts but remains undefined. Is it roughly equivalent to Husserl's sphere of immanence? Plato's forms? Something original? Naming it carefully would clarify almost every argument you're making.
I also need to see the argument for why phenomenological truth and ontological truth are different in kind rather than degree. You assert this repeatedly but the philosophical work establishing it hasn't been done yet.
Concepts to Flesh Out Each Section
For the phenomenological foundation, the most important thing is engaging Husserl's own ladder seriously rather than by reference. Specifically his treatment of intentionality as the structure of consciousness — that consciousness is always of something — would ground your "subjectness" concept precisely. His distinction between noesis and noema, the act of intending versus the intended content, maps almost directly onto what you're trying to establish with experience-as-foundation. Heidegger's concept of thrownness is also relevant here, as it addresses why the subject never encounters the world from a neutral position, which connects to your point about pre-perceptual intuition being contaminated by perceptual knowledge.
For the epistemological middle section, Merleau-Ponty is the figure you're missing most noticeably. His argument that perception is neither purely passive reception nor active construction — that the body is itself a knowing subject — would help you resolve the tension between your sections 1 and 2 about whether sensory properties are learned or foundational. His concept of the motor intentionality would also strengthen section 6 considerably, where you discuss the hand-movement verification example.
For the gap between individual phenomenology and sociology — which is your most underdeveloped bridge — the two most useful resources are Alfred Schutz and early Habermas. Schutz specifically took Husserlian phenomenology and asked what happens when two subjects share a life-world, coining the concept of the "we-relationship" and the "natural attitude" as shared rather than individual. This is almost exactly the bridge you're trying to build intuitively. Habermas's early work on communicative action then asks how intersubjective validity-claims function — what makes a claim count as true or binding across subjects — which connects your epistemological concerns to the social critique.
For the hyperreal and semiotics section, you're clearly drawing on Baudrillard but would benefit from engaging Peirce's semiotic triad more explicitly — sign, object, interpretant — because your "subject-culture coherency drift" is essentially a claim about the collapse of the interpretant's stability under hyperreal conditions. This would give you a more rigorous vocabulary than "coherency drift," precise as that phrase is intuitively.
The Bridge Itself
The conceptual move that would unify everything is one you haven't fully articulated yet: the claim that intersubjectivity is not just a social fact but a phenomenological necessity. If the subject's world is always already structured by shared horizons of meaning — which is Husserl's late position in the Crisis — then the gap between individual phenomenology and sociology isn't a gap to cross but a false division to dissolve. Your societal cognition idea from the first draft was gesturing at this but retreated into metaphor. Making it a philosophical argument rather than an analogy is the single most important structural move available to you.
